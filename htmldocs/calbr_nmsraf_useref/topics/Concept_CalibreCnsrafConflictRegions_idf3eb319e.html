Calibre cnSRAF Conflict Regions

Conflict regions are processed by cnsraf to ensure adherence to MRC requirements and to enable resolution of SRAFs within conflict regions.

Conflict regions are formed by analysis of SRAFs that violate MRC rules. Such a region is depicted in the bounding box in Figure 2 where the spacing of generated SRAFs is less than the required MRC constraint.

A conflict is defined as a region containing two or more SRAFs of the same priority that violate MRC rules. The formation of conflict regions may sometimes be limited by the precision of the output database. SRAFs may be generated with different dimensions than specified in the recipe because of grid snapping, affecting dimensions of sbwidth (scattering bar width) and sraf (square SRAF size). Examples of typical conflict regions are shown in Figure 1.

Figure 1. Typical Conflict Regions



Rather than remove these SRAFs because of exclusion zone violations, conflict regions provide another means to process SRAF placement. The conflict regions are explicitly defined by the bounding box of all contributing SRAFs. Nearby conflict regions are merged, which may result in large areas or complex, non‑rectangular areas.

Conflict regions are, by definition, larger than any contributing SRAF because overlapping SRAFs are not considered to be conflicting. Regions have processing options that provide flexibility over how SRAFs are placed. These processing options control SRAF cleanup, size, orientation, and position. Conflict regions can be ignored if the size constraint or the rectangle requirement is violated. If either of these constraints is violated, no SRAFs are placed within the area and the conflict region is discarded.

Note:

Templates using the dynamicsizing keyword do not form conflict regions.

Figure 2. Conflict Region

SRAFs placed in conflict regions are removed allowing lower priority SRAFs to take precedence in those areas. Conflict regions, however, are either filled by a rectangular SRAF or are completely removed. The following regions are removed for these cases:

  • Regions that are non-rectangular (as a result of merged conflict regions)

  • Conflict regions that violate the MRC rules (to ensure that subsequent SRAFs generation conform to the rules)

  • Conflict regions whose width or height exceed the specified maximum edge length (maxconflict)

When you specify conflictonly within a template, SRAFs are placed if the target shapes are close enough such that conflict regions are formed by SRAFs of the same priority. This is a convenient option when SRAFs in densely-packed regions form higher priority templates than isolated regions. The conflictonly argument and dynamicsizing keyword cannot be used within the same priority. Siemens EDA recommends using conflictonly only when conflict resolution shapes are relatively small and not using it for larger shapes such as line layer designs.

Using centerthresh specifies a threshold for the length and width of a conflict region determined by cnsraf. This keyword consolidates initial SRAFs that are too close together. For more information, see the centerthresh keyword.

Figure 3 shows a rectdiag template with and without the conflictonly argument.

Figure 3. rectdiag With and Without conflictonly

The conflictonly argument permits the template to generate SRAFs only if they form conflicts with SRAFs of the same priority. The left side of Figure 3 uses conflictonly and does not have any such conflicts above the green target shapes, so the red SRAFs are not generated in that space. However, the right side of Figure 3 does not use conflictonly, so the blue SRAFs circled are generated in the upper portion of the illustration.

Further processing of the conflict region depends on the X and Y extents and the centerthresh setting. See centerthresh for more information.
