EUV Modeling Concepts

An EUV system uses a much lower wavelength (in the 13.5 nm range, which is about a 10x reduction in wavelength) compared to a standard system. At this wavelength, optics must be all reflective.

Two EUV-specific effect characteristics must be accounted for:

  • Flare effects, which are caused by scattering from the surface roughness of the mirrors. The flare effects increase with 1/λ2, which causes a loss of image contrast.

  • Shadowing, which is caused by off-axis illumination. Shadowing makes some features print differently, depending on the orientation and position of the slit.

Flare models modulate the intensity from the optical model according to the equation in Figure 1.

Figure 1. Flare Model Equation

  • The total integrated scatter (TIS) gives the fraction of energy lost for image formation due to flare. It is equal to the integral of the flare PSF.

  • I_optical is the optical intensity at location (x,y).

  • I_long and I_short are the long and short range flare respectively.

    The short range flare is calculated by a more specialized simulator in the range of a few hundred nanometers to a few microns (Figure 2). The range from 0 up to the beginning of the short range flare is covered by the optical aberrations, so the optical radius should match this range.

    The long range flare is obtained from the flare map.

  • I_clear is the clear field intensity.

  • PSF_long and PSF_short represent the long range and short range point spread functions.

  • kf is the flare coefficient (usually set to a value of 1).
