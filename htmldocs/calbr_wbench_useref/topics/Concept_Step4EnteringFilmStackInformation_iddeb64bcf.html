Step 4 - Entering Film Stack Information

In this step, you add film stack information to the optical model. Accurate film stack information, such as thickness, refraction, and absorption of all layers, improves the model accuracy.

When the composition of the stack is known, it can be entered explicitly.

If the properties of the film stack are not known, an aerial image model can be created, as described in “Aerial Image Model - Selecting Image Planes Parameters”.

Thin-film Models - Defining the Film Stack and Beamfocus

Usage Notes

Defining Films

  • When multiple films are specified, they are assumed to be stacked downward (in the direction of the propagating light) in the order specified in the table or the Optical file.

    Tip

    The beamfocus level z should always be set to be in the resist layer, regardless of how many TARC layers are above the resist.

  • The beamfocus is always measured relative to the top of the film stack as a distance in the immersion medium or air. When one or more top-layer anti-reflective coating (TARC) layers are placed above the resist layer, the best focus distance z in Figure 1 must be adjusted for each film above and including the resist to convert the beamfocus value.

Setting Beamfocus

Beamfocus is one part of the description of the optical system, on par with such parameters as NA, λ (wavelength), source shape, and film stack properties (resist thickness, n, k). It is closely related to the focus setting on the scanner. Changing the scanner focus is equivalent to changing the beamfocus by the same amount. Since beamfocus corresponds to real movement of the wafer relative to the scanner, it is measured in the immersion medium or air.

To understand beamfocus, consider a well defined image plane to be best focus in the immersion medium or air, that is, the best focus in the absence of the resist film stack. Beamfocus defines the location of this plane of best focus in the immersion medium or air relative to the top of the film stack. In essence, beamfocus describes the location where the scanner is “trying” to focus the image. This is exactly where the in-focus image would be in the absence of the resist film stack, but the presence of the film stack causes the true best focus position inside the resist to shift and not coincide with the best focus in the ambient medium (Figure 2).

  • Negative values for beamfocus indicate that the best focus in the ambient exists at a plane above the top of (outside) the film stack.

  • Positive values for beamfocus indicate that the best focus in the ambient exists at a plane below the top of the film stack.

    Figure 2. Understanding Beamfocus

Note that beamfocus is NOT the location of the best focus of the image formed inside the resist. The position of best focus in the presence of the resist film stack will in general be at z greater than beamfocus, and can only be determined from simulation.

Thin-Film Models - Selecting Image Plane Parameters

The image plane parameters define the output planes where the image is calculated. After the initial model is created, these parameters can be tuned to fit the empirical data from the test pattern.

Follow these steps:

  1. In the Optical Model Tool, switch to the Kernels/Image tab.

  2. Set the three Image parameters according to Table 2.

    The most common practice is to use a single image plane for thin-film models. The latent image inside the resist is calculated either based on the single image plane or by averaging images on multiple planes. Averaging multiple planes using image plane parameters is applicable for older technology generations when the resist thickness was large enough to contain multiple standing waves (see the Usage Notes).

    The current recommendation is to use a single image plane, or “defocus start,” and to account for image blur using image blur parameters described in “Step 7 - Handling Focusblur Information In Optical Models (Optional)”.

  3. Continue to “Step 5 - Adding Pellicle and Jones Pupil Information to the Optical Model (Optional)”.

Tip

The coordinate system for these parameters is slightly different for thin-film models than for aerial image models.

  • Thin-film models — origin = top of the film stack.

  • Models in ambient (Aerial Image) — origin = best focus in ambient.

For aerial images, the top of the resist is not relevant. Also, positive values move in different directions for the two types. In thin-film models, positive values represent planes below the interface, and thus further from the lens. In aerial image models, positive values represent planes above the best focal plane in air, and thus closer to the lens.

Usage Notes

  • Accounting for Refraction

    While vector thin-film models provide the most complete and physical description of image formation inside resist, in some cases scalar aerial image models are an adequate approximation (NA<0.8). You can account for the refraction of the rays at the air/resist interface by adjusting the image plane values to reflect the thickness in the ambient that is mathematically equivalent to the estimated resist thickness.

    Given the refractive index (nresist) and estimated physical thickness of the resist (tphys), the equivalent thickness (tin_ambient) can be calculated using the formula:

    tin_ambient = nambient*tphys/nresist

    Once the equivalent thickness in air (tin_ambient) and the step are known, the number of planes (num) and start can be calculated using the original formula, simply by substituting the equivalent thickness for the physical thickness.
